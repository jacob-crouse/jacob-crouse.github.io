Experience

Penn State UAV Research Laboratory (PURL)

Graduate Research Assistant

Since August, I have enacted both software and hardware changes to multiple unmanned vehicles under the guidance of my advisor, Dr. Eric Johnson.

I outfitted a commercially-available hobby-level fixed wing with electronics necessary for unmanned flight. This included receiver and flight controller setup using open-source flight control software, as well as a wireless communications link for use with a ground station for remote monitoring and commanding of autonomous waypoints.

Additionally, I have developed our own fork of the open-source Betaflight flight control software for multirotor autonomous flight. The fork includes additions of serial read and write commands for sending IMU readings and pilot input data, and auto/manual logic for switching between automatic and manual flight.

To see all the work happening at PURL, check out the lab website.

August 2018 - Present

National Institute of Standards and Technology

Engineering Intern

As an undergraduate, I looked to place myself as quickly as possible into a position where I could experience a professional engineering environment.

As a fresh intern, I spent my first summer becoming familiar with first responders and their missions, problems, and ideas for new technology. This research culminated in my first publication: The Next-Generation Firefighter: The Evolution of Technology.

During the rest of my tenure at NIST, I created technology that would demonstrate potential improvements to the current technology used by first responders. To accomplish this, I created a Lego model of the physical layer of LTE (cellular) downlink to show first responders just how much more advanced LTE is when compared with traditional Land Mobile Radio (LMR). Additionally, I implemented an HTML dashboard which was utilized to display the location and vital statistics of a first responder equipped with a sensor harness to monitor their health.

For me, the choice to attend grad school was simple. Undergrad did a great job of exposing me to all sub-disciplines within aerospace engineering, but left me wanting when it came to improving the depth of my knowledge in areas that especially interested me. By the time senior year came around, I wanted to know more about dynamics and control systems before entering the workforce. If anything, grad school has shown me that there is more to learn than I can possibly imagine, so I look forward to learning as much as possible in the workplace.

As a graduate student, you are responsible for interacting with department faculty much more often than as an undergrad. In this role, I've had the opportunity to become much more familiar with the inner workings of academia, and by extension, the industry that sponsors adademic research projects.

My thesis topic has to do with quadrotor vehicle performance when station keeping over a ship. I have begun work on this topic already by submitting a conference paper to be presented at SciTech 2020 in Orlando.
